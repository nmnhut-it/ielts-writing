Writing Lessons

Strategy, structure, and the exact language that separates Band 5 from Band 7.

Task 1 — Academic Chart/Graph/Table Description

20 minutes. At least 150 words. No opinion — just describe and compare the data.

The 4-paragraph structure every Band-7 answer follows

Intro
Paraphrase the question in 1 sentence. Don't copy the prompt — change at least 3 content words. Example: "The graph illustrates…" → "The chart reveals trends in…"
Overview
Overview paragraph — 2 sentences summarising the 2–3 most striking features. NO numbers here. This is the single most important paragraph; it's worth the largest chunk of your Task Achievement mark.
Body 1
Detail paragraph 1 — 3–4 sentences with specific numbers, covering one half of the data (e.g. the highest/growing categories).
Body 2
Detail paragraph 2 — 3–4 sentences with numbers, covering the other half (e.g. the lowest/declining categories) plus comparison.
Band 7 rule: You MUST have a clear overview paragraph with the word "Overall" (or equivalent). Without it, you cannot score above Band 5 for Task Achievement — this is a hard cap, not a guideline.

How to write the overview

Ignore numbers. Zoom out. What are the 2–3 "headline" facts a reader would notice from 5 metres away?

Template sentences

Overall, it is clear that [biggest trend], while [second trend].
In general, [category] was the most common throughout the period.
Overall, all three categories rose, with [X] showing the steepest increase.
It can be seen that [highest] experienced the largest growth, while [lowest] remained relatively stable.

Upward movement

rise / climb / grow — neutral

Downward movement

fall / drop / decline / decrease
plummet / plunge / collapse — sharp fall

Fluctuation

Approximation

just over / just under
nearly / almost / close to

Worked example — good vs bad overview

Band 5 overview The chart shows household spending from 2015 to 2020. There are three categories: food, housing, and transport. Housing was 800 dollars in January and 830 in June. Food was 320 in January.

Problems: no "headline fact", packed with numbers (numbers belong in body paragraphs), simply repeats the data in the order it appears.

Band 7 overview Overall, it is clear that housing was by far the largest monthly expense throughout the period, while transport consistently accounted for the smallest share. All three categories showed a modest upward trend over the six months.

Why it works: zero numbers, two "headline" comparisons (biggest, smallest), adds one trend observation. Reader understands the whole chart in 2 sentences.

Task 2 — Essay Structure

40 minutes. At least 250 words. Band 7+ candidates typically write 270–310 words. Under-length caps Task Response at Band 5.

The 4-paragraph frame

Intro
2–3 sentences. Paraphrase the question. State your position clearly. (For discussion essays: state that you will discuss both sides and give your view.)
Body 1
4–6 sentences. First main argument. Use the PEEL pattern.
Body 2
4–6 sentences. Second main argument. Same PEEL pattern.
Conclusion
2–3 sentences. Restate your position in new words. Briefly summarise the two main points.

PEEL — the paragraph template

Every body paragraph in Band 7+ writing follows this pattern:

  • P — Point. One clear topic sentence stating the main idea of this paragraph.
  • E — Explanation. Why does this point matter? How does it work? 1–2 sentences.
  • E — Example. A specific, concrete example or piece of evidence. Real or plausible hypothetical.
  • L — Link. Tie the paragraph back to the essay question / your position in one sentence.

PEEL in practice

Band 7 body paragraph (P) The most significant benefit of remote working is the flexibility it offers employees to balance their professional and personal lives. (E) Without the need to commute, workers can reclaim several hours a day, which they can spend on childcare, exercise, or further study — all of which contribute to wellbeing and productivity. (E) A 2023 study by Stanford University, for instance, found that remote workers reported 13% higher job satisfaction than their office-based peers, largely due to the flexibility factor. (L) This flexibility alone is, in my view, a compelling reason to support the expansion of remote working arrangements.

Introduction templates

A reliable 2-sentence intro: paraphrase + position.

Opinion (To what extent do you agree?)

It is often argued that [paraphrased topic]. In my opinion, I largely agree with this view, primarily because [one-sentence reason hinting at your body paragraphs].

Discussion (Discuss both views)

The question of whether [paraphrased topic] has divided opinion. While some argue that [view A], others believe [view B]. This essay will examine both perspectives before concluding that [your view].

Problem-Solution

[Paraphrased problem] has become increasingly pressing in recent years. This essay will first identify the principal causes of this issue before proposing two practical solutions.

Conclusion — what not to write

Weak conclusions repeat the introduction word-for-word. Strong conclusions:

  1. Restate the position using different vocabulary and sentence structure.
  2. Briefly summarise the 2 body arguments (one short clause each).
  3. Optional: end with a forward-looking sentence ("…it is hoped that…", "…further research would…") — gives the essay a sense of closure.
Solid conclusion In conclusion, although concerns about [Y] are legitimate, the benefits of [X] — namely [argument 1] and [argument 2] — outweigh them. Going forward, [mild recommendation] would strike the most sensible balance.

Task 2 — The 5 essay types

Every T2 question fits one of these shapes. Identify it in 10 seconds, then you know exactly how to structure your 4 paragraphs.

1. Opinion — "To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

Your job: Take a clear side (agree / partly agree / disagree) and defend it with 2 reasons.

  • Intro: Paraphrase + state your position (don't sit on the fence).
  • Body 1: First reason you hold that position (PEEL).
  • Body 2: Second reason (PEEL).
  • Conclusion: Restate position, summarise 2 reasons.
Partial agreement is fine — in fact, often stronger. "I largely agree, although with one significant caveat…" lets you use Body 1 for your main agreement and Body 2 for the caveat, showing nuance.

2. Discussion — "Discuss both views and give your own opinion"

Your job: Present both sides fairly, then give your view.

  • Intro: Paraphrase + signal you'll discuss both + state your overall view.
  • Body 1: View A — explain why some people hold it, give an example. End with "However, this view overlooks…" if you disagree.
  • Body 2: View B — same structure. This should usually be the view you personally favour.
  • Conclusion: Summarise both, restate your overall position.
Common trap: students forget to state their own opinion. Your view is worth marks. Use phrases like "In my opinion…", "Personally, I find the second view more convincing because…"

3. Problem-Solution — "What are the causes/reasons? What solutions/measures?"

Your job: Cause(s) in one body paragraph, solution(s) in the other.

  • Intro: Paraphrase the problem, signal you'll discuss causes and solutions.
  • Body 1: Main cause (PEEL). You can include a second cause briefly if the main one doesn't fill a paragraph.
  • Body 2: Practical solution (PEEL). Who should take the action? How would it help?
  • Conclusion: Summarise the main cause + main solution.

4. Two-part — "Why does this happen? What can be done?"

Your job: Two distinct questions. One paragraph each. This is the easiest type — just answer both questions directly.

  • Intro: Paraphrase the topic, signal you'll address both questions.
  • Body 1: Answer question 1 (PEEL).
  • Body 2: Answer question 2 (PEEL).
  • Conclusion: One sentence summarising each answer.

5. Advantages vs. Disadvantages

Your job: Depends on the exact wording.

  • "Discuss the advantages AND disadvantages" — balanced: one para advantages, one para disadvantages, neutral conclusion.
  • "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" — take a side. Similar to opinion essay: one side stronger than the other. Your "weaker" paragraph concedes the opposite side then rebuts it.
Read the question twice. The word "outweigh" completely changes the required structure. Missing this loses major marks on Task Response.

Cohesion & Linking Words

25% of your total Writing mark is Coherence & Cohesion. Cohesion = how well your sentences connect.

Band-7 principle: VARIETY, not quantity

Using "however" five times, or starting every paragraph with "firstly, secondly, thirdly" — these are the signals of a Band 5–6 essay. Band 7+ writers use a range of cohesive devices, and sometimes none at all (when the ideas flow naturally).

Band 5 — repetitive Firstly, education is important. Moreover, it improves lives. Moreover, countries benefit. However, some people disagree. However, they are wrong. In conclusion, education matters.
Band 7 — varied Education is important because it improves individual lives and, in turn, national prosperity. Some critics dispute this, arguing that practical skills matter more — a view that has some merit, but ultimately overlooks the broader benefits of literacy and numeracy.

Reference — the silent cohesive device

Pronouns and substitute words link sentences without overt connectors. Band 8+ writing relies heavily on these:

  • this / these / that / those — refer back to an earlier idea. "Many workers now work from home. This trend…"
  • such — "companies like Google, Amazon, and Apple — such firms…"
  • the latter / the former — refer to two things mentioned previously.
  • do so / do this — substitute for a verb. "Governments should act. Those that fail to do so will…"

Academic Phrases & Hedging

Academic register = formal, cautious, precise. Avoid "I think", "I feel", contractions, and absolute claims.

Giving your opinion

In my view / In my opinion
From my perspective
It seems to me that
I would argue that
Personally, I believe
To my mind

Avoid: "I think" (too informal for Task 2), "I 100% agree" (absolute claims lower your band).

Hedging — sounding careful, not weak

Absolute claims like "this will always happen" are Band-5 mistakes. Band 7+ writers qualify their claims:

Modal verbs

may / might / could
can lead to / tend to
is likely to / is unlikely to

Hedging adverbs

often / sometimes / generally
largely / mostly / broadly
arguably / presumably
in many cases / to some extent
Too absolute Social media always causes depression in young people, and every teenager will be harmed by it.
Properly hedged Social media may contribute to depression in young people, and many teenagers appear to be affected by it.

Presenting arguments

One of the main reasons is that…
A key argument in favour is…
Supporters of this view argue that…
A common counter-argument is that…
Critics, however, point out that…
This view can be challenged on the grounds that…

Common Errors & Fixes

These are the top mistakes I flag most often when marking student essays — fix these and your Band jumps half a point.

1. Overusing "firstly, secondly, thirdly"

Band 5 Firstly, technology helps us communicate. Secondly, it saves time. Thirdly, it is useful for business.
Band 7 The most significant benefit of modern technology is the ease of communication it enables. Beyond this, it dramatically reduces the time needed for routine tasks, which in turn boosts productivity — a particular advantage in business contexts.

Fix: Build sentences with content connectors ("Beyond this…", "A second benefit…", "This in turn…") instead of "Firstly/Secondly".

2. Not paraphrasing the intro

Copying the question verbatim is not counted in your 250-word total and signals poor vocabulary.

Copied from prompt Some people believe that governments should spend more money on public libraries rather than on sports facilities. Some people believe that governments should spend…
Paraphrased Some argue that state funding should prioritise public reading spaces over athletic venues. Others disagree, believing that sport deserves equal or greater investment.

Fix: Change at least 3 content words per sentence. "governments" → "the state / public authorities"; "spend money on" → "allocate funding to / invest in"; "libraries" → "reading spaces / public books collections".

3. Writing "I think" and "In my opinion, I think"

"I think" is too informal for academic writing. "In my opinion, I think" is redundant.

Fix: Use one, and the right one. "In my view," / "I would argue that" / "It seems to me" — any of these, once, per essay.

4. Forgetting the conclusion

Running out of time and skipping the conclusion = automatic Band-5 ceiling on Task Response. The conclusion doesn't have to be long — two sentences is enough.

2-sentence conclusion (works fine) In conclusion, although concerns about X are legitimate, the benefits of Y are more substantial. Governments should therefore continue to support Z while remaining alert to potential risks.

5. Not giving specific examples

Vague body paragraphs kill Task Response marks. "For example, in some countries, people do this" is not an example — it's a placeholder.

No concrete example For example, many countries have laws about this, and it helps society a lot.
Specific example For example, Denmark's bicycle infrastructure, built up since the 1970s, has reduced urban CO₂ emissions by an estimated 20% in cities like Copenhagen.

Fix: Commit to specifics — even plausible hypotheticals are fine. Real statistics, named places, named policies, named companies, named years. IELTS doesn't check your facts; it checks whether you sound authoritative.

6. Repetitive vocabulary

Using the same word 4+ times signals limited Lexical Resource.

Fix: Build synonyms before you start writing. For a common key word from the prompt, know 2–3 alternatives.

IELTS Writing Band Descriptors — The 4 Criteria

Your writing is marked on 4 equally-weighted criteria. The final band is the average. Knowing what each criterion looks for tells you exactly what to work on.

The 4 criteria, at a glance

CriterionWhat it testsTypical band-7 evidence
Task Achievement / Response Have you fully addressed the question, taken a clear position, and developed your ideas? Clear position stated in intro + maintained. All parts of the prompt answered. Specific supporting examples.
Coherence & Cohesion Is the essay logically organised? Are ideas linked smoothly? 4 distinct paragraphs. Clear topic sentences. Varied linking words. Effective reference words (this, such).
Lexical Resource Do you have a wide vocabulary and use words accurately? Less-common words used correctly. Good collocations ("significant impact", "deeply entrenched"). Occasional minor slips OK.
Grammatical Range & Accuracy Do you use a variety of sentence structures, mostly error-free? Mix of simple + complex sentences. Relative clauses, conditionals, passive voice. Frequent error-free sentences.

The under-length rule

If you write fewer than 250 words on Task 2 (or fewer than 150 on Task 1), Task Achievement/Response is capped at Band 5. No matter how good your grammar or vocabulary is, your overall band cannot exceed around 5.5. Always count words as you near the end, and if you're short, add a sentence or two.

What gets a Band 7 vs Band 6

CriterionBand 6 (typical)Band 7 (target)
TA/TR Addresses the task but some parts weakly; position exists but conclusions unclear. All parts addressed; clear position throughout; main ideas extended and supported.
CC Coherent but cohesive devices overused or inaccurate; paragraphing not always logical. Logical organisation, clear progression; cohesive devices used appropriately.
LR Adequate range; attempts less-common words with some inaccuracy. Sufficient range for flexibility and precision; uses less-common items with awareness of style.
GRA Mix of simple + complex; frequent errors in complex forms but rarely impede communication. Variety of complex structures; frequent error-free sentences; good control.
The single biggest lever from 6 to 7: accuracy in complex sentences. A Band 6 writer attempts complex structures but gets errors in them; a Band 7 writer produces frequent error-free complex sentences. Practise specific structures — relative clauses, conditionals, participle phrases — until they're automatic.
